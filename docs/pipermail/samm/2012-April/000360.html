On 6 Apr 2012, at 21:51, Pravir Chandra wrote:

> Now THIS would be awesome. If such a tool could anonymize and 
> accumulate data at project-level granularity with associated project 
> meta-data (perhaps risk-ratings or other architectural 
> characteristics), we'd have a *really* useful dataset.

But this is the whole point of the tool - to collect data, to do what 
follows all requires we have that data… I imagine something where the 
questions are all tied back to a set o practices, which are all tied 
back to a set of activities that produce artefacts. So in a ideal world 
you can have a 'real-time' SAMM assessment, and your score-card becomes 
a dynamic consolidation of the artefacts. This makes it possible to say 
take GIT snapshots of that activity (perhaps daily?) and have a whole 
new host of data that we currently don't have. SAMM is wicked cool, lets 
take it to the next level of cool!

This is roughly what I imagine, assume you had an ISO 27000 audit and 
instead of that being a giant hair-pulling process that takes a year to 
complete it was a matter of sitting down to a web application and asking 
for a report of compliance with evidence and it dynamically generated it 
from a database? (I know, I know - stay with me though...)

Scale that down to SAMM and you have a good idea about what I am 
thinking.

Take for example Under Governance we have Education:
What do we do to educate? How often? Lets say we every quarter we run a 
security awareness campaign that campaign is one of the education 
practices; and lets just further say that this quarter we educated our 
developers on the OWASP top 10.

<tangent>
Now, this is important - we should point back to our own OWASP projects 
as much as possible as suggested practices where possible.
</tangent>

Now the evidence could be that we quizzed all the developers on that 
material and their quizzes, or perhaps their signatures of attendance 
become the evidence of attendance. This evidence now 'moves' the needle 
of maturity in our SAMM rating for Education.

SAMM constitutes a kind of a policy, which needs supporting practices, 
all of which require actives, all of which generate evidence. And right 
now I am not able to capture any of that incredibly rich data…

Imagine further that this is (see turnkey linux) a VM, or an ISO, or XEN 
or EC2 deployable tool; and literally anybody is up and running SAMM!

That would be wicked - ISMS in a box…

> This would address the main reason I think the BSIMM (or if we did 
> them for SAMM) style of stats at the organizational level *can* be 
> harmful. Let me explain. It is an utterly meaningless statement for 
> any organization with a medium to large development staff to say 'We 
> do X.' Take pentesting. <org name> does pen-testing? Really? What 
> projects? 100% of them? 0.01% of them? Now what about frequency... 
> Continuously daily? Every year? Every decade? All I'm saying is that 
> the difference between great and miserable assurance programs all live 
> in that grey area. Further, while I haven't looked at the latest 
> BSIMM, the earlier versions all stated they used the 'high water mark' 
> for scoring a practice. So if you find zero level 1 activities, zero 
> level 2, and a single level 3, then the org gets a 3 score for the 
> whole practice. This further whitewashes over any meaning from the 
> underlying data, but that's a BSIMM problem and not a general problem 
> with measurement at the org level, so I digress.
>
> But project-level granularity of data solves this abstraction problem. 
> Of course for some practices you may want to still measure at the org 
> level instead of project (e.g. policy), but any practice could vary at 
> the project level. I've recommended to almost every organization that 
> they 'slice' their apps/projects on some factor(s). My favorite was 
> 'risk buckets', for instance high/med/low. If a project is in the high 
> category, they have a particular SAMM vector they're expected to meet. 
> For medium, we lower the expected levels. And for low, sometimes we 
> just leave it unspecified or very lightweight (a few level 1's). 
> Instead of slicing on risk buckets, sometimes we've used 
> line-of-business boundaries. Sometimes both. Sometimes other factors 
> (inhouse/outsourced, lots of others potentially).
>
> So don't get me wrong, I'm all for data collection and stats, I'm just 
> weighing in my opinion on the 'right' way to do the measurement. It 
> would be kick-ass if we could say something like: "compared to apps 
> tagged 'internet-facing' and 'financial-services' and 'high-risk', 
> YOUR app score is in the 80th percentile". And if those could be 
> continuously updated from aggregated and anonymized stats? THAT would 
> be killer.
>
> p.
