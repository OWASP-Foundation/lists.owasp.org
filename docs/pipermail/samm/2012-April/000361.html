On Sat, Apr 7, 2012 at 6:51 AM, Pravir Chandra <chandra at owasp.org> wrote:
> This would address the main reason I think the BSIMM (or if we did them
> for SAMM) style of stats at the organizational level *can* be harmful. Let
> me explain. It is an utterly meaningless statement for any organization with
> a medium to large development staff to say 'We do X.' Take pentesting. <org
> name> does pen-testing? Really? What projects? 100% of them? 0.01% of them?
> Now what about frequency... Continuously daily? Every year? Every decade?
> All I'm saying is that the difference between great and miserable assurance
> programs all live in that grey area.

This low level of granularity might not be of interest to an executive
i.e. the intended audience.

It could be of value to others (e.g. middle management) provided the
issue with the frequency metric could be overcome e.g. fuzzing is
conducted as part of continuous integration and if it fails then what
is the effect on the measurement of future builds (e.g. the next build
of continuous integration) if the initial crash is still outstanding?
The answer would be subjective.

On Sat, Apr 7, 2012 at 6:51 AM, Pravir Chandra <chandra at owasp.org> wrote:
> Further, while I haven't looked at the latest BSIMM, the earlier versions all stated
> they used the 'high water mark' for scoring a practice. So if you find zero level 1
> activities, zero level 2, and a single level 3, then the org gets a 3 score for the whole
> practice. This further whitewashes over any meaning from the underlying
> data, but that's a BSIMM problem and not a general problem with measurement
> at the org level, so I digress.

BSIMM reflects that some companies are conducting specific advanced
activities without consideration of the activities at a lower maturity
level and this is explored within their "Ranking BSIMM3 Activities".
Furthermore, BSIMM does not reference
http://www.sei.cmu.edu/cmmi/solutions/dev/index.cfm and this inferred
within http://www.informit.com/articles/article.aspx?p=1562220

Therefore, this issue, in the context of CMMI, is directly related to
OpenSAMM (which BSIMM is based on the beta release) since it does not
reflect real world implementation.

On Sat, Apr 7, 2012 at 6:51 AM, Pravir Chandra <chandra at owasp.org> wrote:
> But project-level granularity of data solves this abstraction problem. Of
> course for some practices you may want to still measure at the org level
> instead of project (e.g. policy), but any practice could vary at the project
> level. I've recommended to almost every organization that they 'slice' their
> apps/projects on some factor(s). My favorite was 'risk buckets', for
> instance high/med/low. If a project is in the high category, they have a
> particular SAMM vector they're expected to meet. For medium, we lower the
> expected levels. And for low, sometimes we just leave it unspecified or very
> lightweight (a few level 1's). Instead of slicing on risk buckets, sometimes
> we've used line-of-business boundaries. Sometimes both. Sometimes other
> factors (inhouse/outsourced, lots of others potentially).

This will result in every application development manager accepting
the lower residual risk to ensure that their application ships without
any security controls so that they get their bonus i.e. this is
similar to the executive ulterior motive explored in the prior
message.
