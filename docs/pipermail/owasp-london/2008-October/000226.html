This got me thinking.  If the COI isn't responsible for maintaing secure government websites, then who is?  I had a good Google around and was directed mainly toward NHCTU resources, which mainly deal with incidents posthumously.

Is it really the case that on one hand, the COI is responsible for information, yet another department - MI5 or 'the police' are responsible for securing it?

The only true regulation of government websites is the 7th Data Protection Principle of the DPA, which appears humbly in the COI's list of 'legal issues' - http://www.coi.gov.uk/guidance.php?page=164.  This recommends publishing organisations to take a 'risk-based approach to security matters'.  So, whilst the over-riding objective of the COI is to publish information and ensure everyone can see it, they still have the obligation to secure it.

The government takes a statistical approach to security matters - if it's happened before, it's likely to happen again.  If USB sticks go missing, government introduces a re-clarification of what was already hidden in 3 or 400 pages of security policy and mandates USB device encryption (or prohibition).  If backup disks go missing, government tightens up 3rd party contracts and sacks a few suppliers.  If a government web site gets hacked, they fix it.  Quietly...

Being in the business, I know that each and every government web site gets thoroughly pen tested before release by a CHECK member, and re-tested annually.  I'd certainly take it as read that the websites the COI is attempting to standardize are already highly secure and the main problem that's trying to be addressed is that they were all coded to work with IE 6 (which, to say the least, didn't always understand HTML).
