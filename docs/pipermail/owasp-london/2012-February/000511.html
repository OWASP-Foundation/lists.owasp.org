Dinis and Dennis,

I believe
http://www.cigital.com/justice-league-blog/2011/08/09/building-versus-breaking-a-white-hat-goes-to-blackhat/provides
the context of Cigital's viewpoint.

On Fri, Feb 3, 2012 at 6:38 AM, Paco Hope <Paco at cigital.com> wrote:

> The horse is still breathing… just a few more whacks should do…
>
> since when learning how to hack and exploit security issues is bad
> examples?
>
> Teaching hacking and exploiting requires the presence of bad code. And
> that's the whole point. Learning to exploit SQL injection requires us to
> show students software that is missing everything we want them to learn.
> Knowing that the software is broken does not inform us on what would fix
> it. It simply tells us that we must fix it. How one writes software
> correctly is an entirely different question.
>
> It takes real skill, focus, determination, knowledge and passion to find
> and exploit those issues. It's much more like detective work.
>
> Teaching the right way to do things is orthogonal to teaching them passion
> and craftsmanship. You can be passionate about making code that is very,
> very correct. Or you can be passionate about breaking and exploiting
> things. One need not sacrifice passion and craftsmanship in order to teach
> doing the right thing.
>
> We are discussing what to do with an ICT curriculum in schools, when youth
> have little to no computer science or development background. They need to
> learn the correct thing first.
>
> NO, absolutely not.
>
> They need to learn passion and love and craftsmanship first.
>
> I respect this and I agree. I just don't see how hacking and exploiting is
> craftsmanship, whereas building correct software correctly is not. Nor do I
> see how one teaches either of these things (correctness or passion) to the
> exclusion of the other. The two things are unrelated. For every hacker you
> can point to who is passionate about his craft, I can find an equally
> talented developer whose code is solid because he is passionate about doing
> it right. There is no opposition between these two goals.
>
> Consider spelling. One does not teach spelling by providing a long list of
> misspelled words to children who don't yet know how to spell.
>
> sorry, but actually you do :)
>
> I have a 6 and 8 year old and just saw this happening in front of me. In
> the beginning the teachers are much more focused on getting the kid
> interested in reading books, and they are not that bothered if the kid
> reads the wrong word. The key is that the kid learns to like reading.
>
> We have kids the same ages. When they practice reading, we are putting
> correctly spelled words in front of them over and over and over again. We
> don't teach them spelling at all until after they have spent years
> (hopefully) looking at lots and lots of correctly spelled words.
>
> Correct spelling actually comes after reading, where once the kid starts
> to learn how to sound things, he/she is thought the gramatical rules and
> eventually have speling tests on blocks of words. Even then, the teachers
> will reward kids that make good efforts and write down a spelling that
> phonetically is equivalent
>
> Again, no disagreement. But my oldest has not yet come home with a
> homework assignment saying "Some of these words are misspelled. Which
> ones?" Nor has he come home with a sheet of incorrect and correct words,
> where the incorrectly spelled ones are identified for him. We start putting
> a mixture of correctly and incorrectly spelled words in front of children
> only after years of exclusively showing them correctly spelled words.
> That's educational theory being put to practice.
>
> That is what teaching hacking would be. It's putting good and bad software
> in front of a student and asking them to see the distinction. When they're
> in secondary school or university, they don't have that capability, yet.
> They don't know enough about software to understand which bits are good and
> which are bad.
>
> We shouldn't show students broken software and talk about fixing it, until
> we have shown them lots and lots of good software and good practices they
> should emulate. Bad software is everywhere. Good software, that we might
> point to and say "go do it that way", is in desperately short supply.
>
> Note that I had a LOT of teachers at university and college that used
> those techniques, and I learned more about computers trying to write C++
> and Assembly to play tricks on my colleges, than I ever did via the
> 'building good foundations' way
>
> I obviously can't argue what worked for you. What I appeal to is
> educational theory. Most people fall under the wide part of the bell curve
> and they need something a lot more basic that gets them good enough.
> Educational theory, which is all about doing the right thing for the
> majority, says you teach the right thing first.
>
>
> Hacking and exploiting are cool in the way that graffiti and vandalism are
> cool.
>
> That is your pre-conceived ideas of hacking, and maybe this is where we
> have to part ways.
>
> If you study history you will see that most computing advances were
> created by hacking activities, and reducing it to 'vandalism' is bringing
> down this conversation to a very low level.
>
> If we're going to quote movies, like Dennis did, I'll quote Star Wars:
> "Stay on target… stay on target…". I'm not suggesting things that will
> advance the field nor am I putting up stumbling blocks for those who would.
> I am answering the question of what we should teach everyone. What is the
> baseline we teach to? Hacking and exploitation are, as I said, destructive.
> They are not the kind of thing you teach everyone because they don't lead
> you to creating better software in the first place.
>
> You cannot 'hack' me an enterprise-grade transaction processing system
> that meets stringent standards of reliability, accuracy, performance,
> security, etc. If you produce one of those things, then the activity you're
> doing is not 'hacking'. You cannot 'exploit' your way to a robust auth/auth
> system. If you produce a valid, correct, and reliable auth/auth system, you
> do it through processes that no one would call 'exploitation'. Thus if the
> goal we are trying to achieve is producing correct software that is secure,
> reliable, and meeting business goals, then teaching 'hacking' and
> 'exploiting' would be teaching activities that do not lead to that result.
>
>
> They are destructive activities that cannot, on their own, create
> something.
>
> I believe in science they call this 'experimentation' , 'validation' and
> 'peer-review'.
>
> Those are fine processes. You neither 'validate' nor 'peer review' in
> order to create. Maybe a more palatable word is to say they refine, rather
> than destroy. Either way, they don't help the creator on his first draft.
> They only help the creator in subsequent drafts (be that a scientific
> paper, or software). We need to help people make their first draft better.
>
> Experimentation is fine, but we're not suffering from a shortage of
> experimental, half-baked software. We're suffering from a shortage of
> rock-solid, mission-accomplishing, reliable, secure software. I don't want
> to dampen anyone's creative spirit. But when it's time to move that idea
> out of the garage and onto the market, it has to be built differently. We
> need to know that the average person you're interviewing, who came through
> a regular ICT program, will have a clue about building software well.
>
> The original question was around what should be taught to every person
> learning ICT. I still maintain that hacking and exploitation have little
> value there. The majority of average people in the middle of the bell curve
> will gain nothing by it.
>
> People need to learn to create well. That, indeed, is craftsmanship.
> Loving to create great and beautiful things is a very good goal. Learning
> how beautiful things can be exploited or ruined is of little value in
> achieving that goal. It is not something everyone needs to learn.
>
