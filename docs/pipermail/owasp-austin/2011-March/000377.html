I'm really enjoying this conversation. The articles James used to start it
are great, especially since I've been closed up in my own little world for a
while and didn't really know what was going on in the OWASP community. I
also think the points made by each of the responses in this thread are solid
and have made me think about how we can all work together, especially in
Austin.

I think this issue that the OWASP community as a whole is dealing with is a
perpetual challenge and an important one for the InfoSec community at large.
In communicating the value of security and my services to customers in the
past, I've fallen into the trap of assuming I know the business or the work
or the software better than my target audience. Even if I did know more, the
lack of respect and humility in my language disconnected my audience
immediately. I became a critic at best and an adversary at worst, when what
I really wanted was to work together to build a shared vision and accomplish
a shared goal. I hope that I've grown from those experiences. Now, I'm
trying hard to craft my leadership in a style described thousands of years
ago... "A leader is best when people barely know he exists, when his work is
done, his aim fulfilled, they will say: we did it ourselves." - Lao Tzu


Phil

>> IMHO, OWASP is for everyone.  As our local chapter President, I've strived
>> to find presentations that are as entertaining to auditors as they are
>> developers.  Certainly not an easy thing to accomplish, but I think we've
>> struck a good balance.
>>
>> The notion that "Developers don't know <expletive deleted> about security"
>> is false on so many levels as are most generalizations.  Developers learn
>> what they are taught in school, what they garner through experience, and
>> what they seek out on their own.  As OWASP, it is our job to not only be the
>> place developers feel comfortable seeking out, but to also help to
>> evangelize security within the community and get developers more interested.
>>  Messages like the one above, as Rich said, are divisive at best and
>> undermine OWASP's core mission of educating the masses on security.
>>
>> I'd like to put this out there for everyone in our Austin OWASP community.
>>  Build bridges, not walls.  Make the developers your friends.  Put yourself
>> in the trenches with them and hold regular office hours.  Show enthusiasm
>> and let them know that security is FUN.  Remember that people are motivated
>> by the things *they* want to do, not the things you want them to do.
>>
>> If you haven't done so already, read "How to Win Friends and Influence

>> interactions with people and I believe I've been a better security
