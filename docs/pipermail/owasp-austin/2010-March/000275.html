---- travis+ml-owasp at subspacefield.org wrote: 

> On the topic of crypto, cypherpunks, and privacy, Steven Levy put out
> a book called "Crypto" that is supposed to deal with the topic.  I
> have a copy but haven't had a chance to read it yet.

If you're interested in crypto then get Koblitz's "A Course in Number Theory and Cryptography" and Schnier's "Applied Cryptography". They're both pretty old now, but the basic math hasn't changed a lot. I also very much like Stoll's "The Cuckoo's Egg" as I was hacking around with the Black Monks from Amsterdam on Amigas and there was a constant buzz about the CCC goings on. I got to chat with Pengo on a bridge for about a minute once. I still regularly use my 2nd edition red covered "Applied Cryptography" for a shibboleth for meetings (like Hacking Society).

> Matt Blaze also did an interesting Silver Bullet podcast that covered
> the issue.  What I remember of what he said is that the movement
> started when it became feasible to start doing crypto in software at
> no marginal cost.  Until that time, most crypto was done in
> special-purpose hardware units that were expensive and under tighter
> control.

Matt and I don't see eye to eye on many things. Crypto has been around for quite a while, Khan's book "The Codebreakers" and the publishing of the knapsack algorithms in the 70's is really what kicked off a wave of civilian interest. At this same time it was clear through the growth of the Arpanet that there was going to be a use for this technology. We used to have a hands-on science museum in Austin called Discovery Hall. I was one of the founders along with some others including Dr. Jack Turner ( http://order.ph.utexas.edu/ ) and took care of their BBS and computer SIG among other things. We did a project one summer doing stego on a C64 using the LSB and some XOR padding to whiten the bit sequence (86 I think). What Matt's talking about is probably true for a lot of military and high end commercial apps, but most of it was more algorithmic and done on the Nova or 360 that the business had.

I still have the floppy for the PGP 1.0 that I downloaded to my Amiga from Adelante BBS when Zimmerman put it up that first night. Bass-o-matic :)
